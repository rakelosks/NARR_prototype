City Finances Peaked Fast and Then Plunged After 2007

How the city manages its money affects everything from public services to local investments. After rising steadily and reaching a high point in 2007, the city’s finances dropped sharply—falling by more than 80% from that peak. That big swing has shaped what the city can do ever since.

Story

84%drop in city funds after 2007

After hitting a record high in 2007, the city’s available funds fell by more than four-fifths.

A Sharp Rise, Then a Sudden Fall

In the chart below, you’ll see lines tracing the city’s finances from 2002 to 2017. There’s steady growth at first, reaching a peak in 2007—but then a dramatic drop. By 2017, the main city fund was down 84% from its high point. This shows how quickly big changes can happen in the city’s financial outlook.

Recent Budget Shows a Wide Gap Between Spending and Investment

Looking at the most recent year, the bar chart below lines up different parts of the budget. One group—operating cash flow—stands out as much higher than what the city spent on new long-term projects. This gap means the city’s day-to-day funds far outweighed its investments in things like buildings or roads for that year.

Different Types of Spending Have Shifted Over Time

The final chart shows how each group has grown or shrunk across the years. Some areas, like long-term loan payments or profits from asset sales, moved up or down a lot. This mix reveals how shifting priorities and outside factors have shaped the city’s ability to provide services and invest in new projects.

What This Means for Residents

Big swings in the city’s finances affect the services and improvements people see every day. Sharp drops can mean tougher choices and fewer upgrades, while years with extra funds open up more opportunities. Understanding these changes helps us see why some investments are quick to happen—while others take longer.

City Finances Hit Record High Before Sharp Drop

Many people wonder how the city's finances have shifted over time, especially when making decisions at home can feel tight. The numbers reveal that city funds soared to record heights in 2007, before dropping sharply—falling by more than 80% over the next decade.

Story

84%decline after 2007 peak

The city's available funds dropped by more than four-fifths from their highest point in 2007.

A Dramatic Rise and Fall in City Funds

In the chart below, you can see how the city's total funds grew quickly from 2002, peaking in 2007. After that, the line drops steeply, showing that by 2017, funds had fallen to just a fraction of their earlier high. This swing is rare—funds rose nearly fivefold and then dropped by more than 80% after the peak.

How 2017 Compares Across Spending Categories

Looking at the bar chart below for the latest year, you'll see how different budget categories stack up. The biggest gap is between money from operations—over 24 billion higher than what was spent on long-term investments. Other categories, like new long-term loans and pension commitments, also stand out. This makes it clear where the city's priorities and challenges lie today.

Where All The Money Went Over Time

The final area chart shows not only the total size of the city's finances, but also which categories grew or shrank the most. For example, spending on permanent assets and long-term loans have both changed a lot since 2002, shifting the balance of the city’s overall cash flow.

What This Means for Residents

These highs and lows in city finances shape what the city can offer—from services to new investments. For residents, big swings in funding can mean more uncertainty about future projects or services. Understanding this helps everyone see how the city must plan for both growth and sudden changes.

Data note

This story is based on city finance numbers from 2002 to 2017, measured in thousands of króna. It covers the rise, peak, and sharp decline during this period, but doesn't include anything after 2017.
