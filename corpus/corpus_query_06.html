Traffic Accidents in Reykjavík Almost Halved Since 2000

Many drivers worry about road safety, especially as the city grows. But here's some good news: the number of traffic accidents per 1,000 vehicles in Reykjavík dropped by nearly half in the last 20 years. Even with more cars on the road, crashes are much less common than they used to be.

Story

47%fewer accidents per 1,000 cars since 2000

There's been a big drop in accidents, even though the number of vehicles has gone up.

Steep Drop in Accident Rates Over Time

In the chart below, you can see how the number of accidents per 1,000 vehicles starts out high in 2000, then falls sharply after 2001. By 2019, the rate is almost 50% lower than where it began. The curve dips noticeably around 2008 and stays much lower through the last decade.

Refers to chart index 0

There Are More Cars—But Fewer Accidents per Car

The next chart shows that the total number of vehicles on the road grew by more than half from 2000 to 2019. It peaks around 2007, then drops a little before rising again. Despite this big increase in traffic, accident rates per vehicle have steadily gone down.

Refers to chart index 1

Injuries from Accidents Are Down Too

Take a look at this chart. It shows injuries from accidents per 1,000 vehicles. Like the accident rate, injury numbers also fall sharply over the years, dropping by about two-thirds. This suggests that not only are crashes less frequent, but fewer people are getting hurt as well.

Refers to chart index 3

What This Means for Drivers and Families

For people moving around Reykjavík, these trends mean roads have become safer than they were two decades ago. Fewer crashes and injuries help everyone feel more secure, whether you're driving, cycling, or walking through the city.

Data note

This story uses traffic accident numbers from 2000 to 2019 in Reykjavík, based on police records. The figures do not include minor property-damage crashes that people report directly to insurance companies, so some incidents may not be counted. The most recent year in the data is 2019. Licence information for these numbers was not published.

Serious Traffic Accidents in Reykjavík Have Plummeted

Everyone wants to feel safer on the road, whether driving or walking. The numbers from the past twenty years tell a surprisingly hopeful story: traffic accidents with injuries or deaths have dropped by almost half since the early 2000s, and serious injuries are much rarer than before.

Story

65%fewer injuries per vehicle since 2000

People are much less likely to be hurt in a car crash than they were twenty years ago.

Fewer Accidents Per Car Than Ever Before

In the chart below, you can follow how accidents per thousand vehicles have changed since 2000. In the early 2000s, the rate was almost 50 accidents per 1,000 cars, but by 2019 it had dropped to around 26. That’s about half as many accidents per car as there used to be.

Refers to chart index 0

Number of Cars Rose, Then Fell—But Accidents Still Dropped

Take a look at how the total number of vehicles on the road changed over time. The number climbed quickly at first, reaching a peak in 2007, before dropping by about 15% and then leveling off. Even though there have been ups and downs in how many cars we have, the steady fall in accident rates suggests drivers are sharing the road more safely.

Refers to chart index 1

Sharp Fall in Injuries and Deadly Crashes

The next chart shows how injuries in traffic accidents have dropped over time. In 2000, there were nearly 8 injuries per 1,000 vehicles. By 2019, it was down to less than 3. Deaths from traffic accidents also went from several each year at the start of the period to none in the most recent years. For local families, this means far fewer lives interrupted by tragedy.

Refers to chart index 3

Life on the Streets Feels Safer

This steady drop in accidents and injuries means roads in Reykjavík are safer for all of us. Fewer serious crashes can give peace of mind to parents, cyclists, and drivers alike. While there will always be risks, the numbers show that city life is becoming a little less dangerous every year.

Data note

This story is based on numbers reported by Reykjavík police from 2000 to 2019. The data does not include minor property-only accidents that drivers reported directly to their insurance, so the true number of all fender-benders is higher. Also, licence information for this data could not be fully confirmed. These numbers mostly reflect the most serious collisions—the ones that matter most for public safety.
