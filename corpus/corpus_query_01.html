Residents Throw Away a Third Less Waste Than 15 Years Ago

Most households set out their bins every week, but the numbers reveal something surprising: we’re now tossing out much less than before. In Reykjavík, the amount of household waste per person has dropped by about a third since 1998—falling from 266 kilos a year to just 173 kilos by 2012.

Story

35% lesswaste per person since 1998

Each person in Reykjavík is now throwing away about a third less than they did 15 years ago.

Waste Per Person Drops After Peaking in 2000

In the chart below, you'll see how waste collected per person crept up to a high of 273 kilos around the year 2000. After that, there’s a steady and sharp decline right through to 2012. By the end of this period, the average resident is producing 37% less waste than at the peak. This is the most dramatic shift in the numbers.

Refers to chart index 1

Number of Collection Bins Also Decreases

Look next at the chart showing how many waste bins the city had to empty each year. The number of bins went up until 2004, reaching a high point, but then fell by about a fifth over the next eight years. By 2012, crews were collecting from nearly 11,000 fewer bins than at the peak. This fall matches the drop in waste per person and shows a real change in how much is being thrown away.

Refers to chart index 0

What This Means for Residents

Filling your bin less means less waste picked up, fewer trips for city trucks, and likely lower costs for everyone. Seeing our habits shift so much in just over a decade hints at big changes in what—and how much—we use and throw away. For households, it means we’re all playing a part in making Reykjavík a bit cleaner and less wasteful.

Data note

This story covers waste collection in Reykjavík households from 1998 to 2012. All numbers are citywide averages—your home's habits may differ. The figures come from the city's Environment and Planning division, but licence details are not specified, and costs after 2012 aren't included. Finally, big changes in 2004 and 2005 may be due to policy shifts, so keep in mind that the trends shown reflect both resident behavior and city practices.

Residents Throw Away a Third Less Than 15 Years Ago

Living in Reykjavík means keeping an eye on the waste bins. But fewer bins and less rubbish are filling up the streets these days. Over the past 15 years, the amount of household waste per person has dropped by about 35%, falling from 266 kg in 1998 to just 173 kg in 2012.

Story

35%less waste per person

Since 1998, the amount each resident throws away has dropped by about a third.

Households Are Using Fewer Bins

In the chart below, you’ll notice the number of waste bins across the city steadily rises to a peak in 2004 and then falls sharply. There were about 47,200 bins in 1998, peaking around 47,900 in 2004. But after that, the number dropped by about a fifth, landing at just over 37,300 bins in 2012. This shows that not only are people throwing away less, but fewer bins are needed for the job.

Refers to chart index 0

Big Drop in Waste Per Person After 2000

The next chart shows how much rubbish each person in Reykjavík put out each year. It hit its highest point in 2000 at 273 kg per person, but then dropped sharply. By 2012, that number was down to 173 kg—about a third less than before. This steady decline after 2000 stands out, showing a clear shift toward throwing away less.

Refers to chart index 1

What This Means for Daily Life

For residents, these changes translate to less waste being carried out each week. Fewer bins and lighter loads are a sign that habits are shifting—whether from recycling, reuse, or simply wasting less. All these small efforts together have made a real difference citywide.

Data note

These numbers cover 1998 to 2012, so changes since then aren’t included. The figures reflect the whole city, not specific neighborhoods. There are some changes in how bins were counted, and some waste collection costs may not be fully reflected for the last year. Reykjavik's environmental and planning department collected this information, but the open data doesn’t list a licence.
