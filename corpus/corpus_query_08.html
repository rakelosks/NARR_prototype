Unemployment Still High, but Lower Than Its Peak Year

Many people have felt the stress of job loss in the past years, but the city is no longer at the worst point. In fact, the number of people out of work is almost a third lower than the peak a decade ago.

Story

56,400people unemployed in the most recent year

That's down nearly 30% from the peak in 2009, when almost 79,100 were unemployed.

Unemployment Rose Sharply, Then Fell After 2009

In the chart below, you can see how the line climbs steeply in 2009, with unemployment hitting its highest point. After that year, the number of people out of work slowly decreased, dropping nearly a third by 2020. Even though unemployment has not returned to the lowest early 2000s levels, the trend since the peak has been downward.

Some Months Are Far Harder Than Others

The bar chart here shows big differences between months. For instance, in 2019, April had over 19,000 people unemployed, while September had just over 3,000. That’s more than a fivefold difference. Such swings mean the challenges are not spread out evenly through the year.

Unemployment Adds Up Over Time

Looking at the total across all these years, the area chart below shows how job loss has built up. Most of the rise happened quickly around 2009, with a slower pace since. This means that while the shock was sharp, the effects have lasted.

What This Means for Workers and Families

While fewer people are out of work now than during the worst years, joblessness is still high compared to the early 2000s. This ongoing challenge affects families and shapes life in the city. But the steady drop since the peak offers hope that recovery is possible.

Data note

This data tracks the number of people unemployed in Reykjavík every month from 2000 to 2020, including how long they have been without work. It shows totals for short-term and long-term unemployment. The numbers are for the whole city and may not reflect every neighborhood. Recent years beyond 2020 are not included. Please note, we don't have full licence information for this data, so its use may be limited.

Unemployment Fell Sharply After a 2009 Peak

Unemployment is something many worry about, especially when the numbers start to climb. In Reykjavík, unemployment soared after 2008 but has come down noticeably since its peak in 2009. Even though the numbers are still higher than they were twenty years ago, the latest figures show a big drop from those crisis heights.

Story

29%drop in total unemployment since 2009

Unemployment numbers fell by nearly a third from their highest point in 2009 through to 2020.

Unemployment Rose, Peaked, and Then Dropped

In the line chart below, you can see how unemployment rose steeply from 2007, reached a high in 2009, and then began to fall. The number of people looking for work in Reykjavík went from just over 8,000 in 2007 up to around 79,000 in 2009. Since then, it's dropped 29% to about 56,000 in 2020. The past five years have seen total unemployment stay above early 2000s levels but much lower than that peak.

Big Differences Across Months

The bar chart below shows how unemployment numbers differ from month to month in recent years. For example, April 2019 had far more people without work compared to months like September or December. In April, about 19,311 people were unemployed, while in September it was closer to 3,130. This gap suggests seasonal effects, with some months always standing out with much higher numbers.

Short-term Unemployment Makes up Most of the Numbers

In the area chart below, you can see how much of the total is made up by people out of work less than six months. This group has grown a lot over two decades, but its size has gone down since the 2009 peak, mirroring the bigger trend. Most people who are out of work are looking for a job for less than half a year, especially in these last years.

What this Means for Residents

For most people in Reykjavík, the job market feels steadier now than it did after the big spike in 2009. While unemployment is still higher than it was in the early 2000s, the numbers have moved in the right direction overall. This means a better outlook for those entering the job market or worried about finding work.
