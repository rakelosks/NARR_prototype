Number of Young Children in Single-Parent Families Has Dropped Since 2002

Single-parent families are a vital part of life for many in Reykjavík, shaping everything from schools to playgrounds. Over the past decade, there have been fewer young children living with single parents—especially among the youngest age groups. For instance, the number of 0–2 year olds in these families dropped by about 16% since 2002.

Story

16%fewer 0–2 year olds in 2014 vs. 2002

This is the steepest drop among all age groups of children in single-parent families.

The Biggest Drop Is Among the Youngest Children

In the chart below, you’ll see how the number of very young children (ages 0–2) in single-parent families started high in 2002 but then fell sharply. The numbers peaked early and then declined steadily for more than a decade—about one in six fewer infants and toddlers by 2014. This points to smaller numbers of the youngest children in these families over time.

Look at the next chart. It compares different parts of Reykjavík and shows big gaps between areas. Some districts have many more children in single-parent families than others. For example, Reykjavík overall has much higher numbers than neighborhoods with very few children in this group. This suggests where resources might be needed most.

Not All Age Groups Are Falling at the Same Rate

The heatmap below shows which districts and years saw the biggest changes. You’ll notice that while most places saw drops among the youngest children, there are spots and years where numbers held steady or even went up for older kids. These differences can matter for planning services like after-school programs and community support.

What This Means for Families Around the City

For many residents, these shifts in family makeup can influence everything from preschool spots to housing and support services. Fewer very young children in single-parent homes may mean some neighborhoods feel less crowded in nurseries, while others might see smaller changes. These are the trends shaping daily life for parents and children now and in the years ahead.

Data note

This story is based on data from 2002 to 2014, showing the number of children of different ages in single-parent families by district in Reykjavík. The numbers are for January each year and come from the national statistics office. It does not include children on kindergarten waiting lists or currently in preschools, and only covers up to 2014, so patterns may have changed since then. The licence information for the data was not available at the source.
