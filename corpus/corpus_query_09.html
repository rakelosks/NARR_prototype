Number of Foreign Citizens in Reykjavík Has Soared Since 1998

Reykjavík’s neighborhoods are more international than ever. Over the last two decades, the number of foreign citizens living in the city jumped from just over 3,100 to nearly 24,000. That’s a rise that touches every corner of the city, with some districts seeing especially dramatic growth.

Story

3,222foreign citizens in Efra - Breiðholt (2019)

In 2019, Efra - Breiðholt had more foreign citizens than any other neighborhood in Reykjavík.

Eight Times More Foreign Citizens Citywide

In the chart below, you can see how the number of foreign citizens in Reykjavík has risen year by year. The line climbs steadily, showing that today’s city is much more diverse than it was in the late 1990s. In 1998, there were just over 3,100 foreign citizens. By 2019, that number had soared to almost 24,000—an increase of over eight times.

Efra - Breiðholt Stands Out From the Rest

The next chart compares neighborhoods for the latest year. Efra - Breiðholt stands out as the area with by far the most foreign citizens—3,222 in 2019, well ahead of other districts. Nearby neighborhoods like Breiðholt - Bakkar and Gamli Vesturbærinn also have high numbers but fall far behind Efra - Breiðholt. This big gap shows that some residents share streets with many newcomers, while others live in areas with fewer foreign-born neighbors.

Percentage of Foreign Citizens Keeps Climbing

The following bar chart shows the share of foreign citizens as part of the total population over time. The percentage has risen sharply, from about 3% in 1998 to nearly 19% by 2019. That means almost one in five Reykjavík residents was a foreign citizen by the end of the period.

What This Means for Residents

Walking through Reykjavík today, you’re much more likely to hear languages and see faces from all over the world. Some districts have become real hubs for international communities. This change brings new perspectives, cultures, and connections to the everyday life of the city.

Data note

These numbers come from Iceland's official statistics for 1998 through 2019, so they show changes over twenty years but do not include more recent years. The data reflects foreign citizen numbers and population for all Reykjavík neighborhoods.
