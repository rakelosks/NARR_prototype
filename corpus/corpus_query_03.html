City Air Pollution Levels Have Dropped Dramatically Since the 1990s

Breathing easier isn’t just a feeling—it's backed up by the numbers. Over the past 20 years, pollution in Reykjavík has fallen by about three quarters. In 1994, total measured pollution was 134 micrograms per cubic meter, but by 2016, it was down to just 34.

Story

75%drop in air pollution since 1994

Air quality readings show pollution levels are now just a quarter of what they were in the mid-90s.

Pollution Peaked Before Quickly Falling

In the chart below, you’ll see how city air pollution hit its highest point in 1994, then sank sharply. By 2016, the overall readings were down nearly 75%. This suggests big improvements over the years, with citywide efforts making a visible impact.

Refers to chart index 0

Some Pollutants Stand Out From the Rest

The bar chart compares pollution averages between different substances. For instance, ozone levels were higher than other types like carbon monoxide or nitrogen dioxide. This shows that while most pollutants dropped, some—like ozone—stood apart and deserve special attention.

Refers to chart index 1

Certain Pollutants Changed More Than Others

The heatmap here reveals how quickly some specific types fell. Nitrogen dioxide, for example, dropped from the highest early readings to much lower levels, while ozone increased over time. Seasonal patterns in the area chart suggest peaks during certain months, so it's not always the same story throughout the year.

Refers to chart index 2

Cleaner Air Means Better Health for Everyone

These improvements mean fewer pollutants in the air we all breathe, which is good news for families, runners, and anyone who likes a walk outside. While some challenges remain—like rising ozone—most measures are moving the right way. It’s a positive shift that everyone living here feels, whether they notice it or not.

Refers to chart index 3

Data note

This story is based on pollution measurements from Grensásvegur in Reykjavík, covering years 1994 to 2016. The numbers include six main types of air pollutants. Some years, like 2010 and 2016, had issues with ozone readings. Data comes from city and environmental authorities, but licensing details are missing from the source. Conditions can vary across different parts of the city, so these numbers reflect the situation at the monitoring site.

Air Pollution Has Dropped Sharply Since the 1990s

Many residents worry about the air they breathe, especially during still winter days. But the long-term numbers offer some good news: air pollution levels at Grensásvegur have dropped by about 75% since their peak in 1994.

Story

75%drop in pollution since 1994

Air quality has improved a lot since the mid-90s, with pollution levels now a quarter of what they used to be.

A Sharp Drop After the 90s

In the chart below, you can see how air pollution peaked in the 1990s, then fell steeply. By 2016, total concentrations were down from 134 to 34 micrograms per cubic meter—a huge improvement. Look for the steep decline after the early years.

Refers to chart index 0

Ozone Is Going Up, Others Are Down

The next chart compares all main pollutants. Most harmful substances—like nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide, and dust—have dropped since the mid-90s. Ozone, however, is showing the opposite trend and has risen over time. You’ll see this gap clearly in the bar chart, where ozone stands out as the only one going up.

Refers to chart index 1

Some Pollutants Varied Over the Years

See the heatmap below to get a feel for which years and which pollutants hit their high points. Nitrogen dioxide and dust were once much higher, but their hot spots fade in recent years. Ozone's steady climb also shows up here, staying strong even as others drop off.

Refers to chart index 2

Seasons Matter—But the Trend Remains Clear

The area chart shows how pollution can rise and fall depending on the season or year, but the biggest story is the steady progress overall. Air is cleaner now than it was twenty years ago, even if there are some ups and downs along the way.

Refers to chart index 3

What This Means for Your Health

Breathing cleaner air helps everyone—especially children, the elderly, and people with allergies or asthma. Even though ozone has gone up, the big drop in other pollutants means Reykjavík’s air is much safer today than a generation ago. It's a reminder of how long-term efforts can make a real difference in daily life.

Data note

This story is based on air quality measurements from Grensásvegur, covering the years 1994 to 2016. The numbers show averages across all pollutants recorded at this station; your local air may differ. Ozone readings were not available for 2010 and may be unreliable in 2016. The figures come from Reykjavík's environmental department, but no official licence information is provided, so this public summary is for general information only.
